    Dave did training a couple of weeks ago 

    Formative assessments that built on each other were difficult to keep separate mentally, would like to do a little more preparation to avoid squishing those together 

    About 14 participants, all but 1 completed all modules

    Amanda: Were they talkative, engaged? 

    Dave: Were on the quieter side but there were good discussion at parts 

    One person seemed to be there more for teaching, the rest were more there for the Carpentries, and the one who was there more for teaching maybe felt misled; but the learner centered teaching curriculum should help with this 

    Jon & Amanda - two day training, had ~14 learners

    Pretty engaged, 1 person missed a bit of time but most didn't

    Feedback: 1 person was saying it was a very auditory training and not very amenable to other learning styles 

    We dealt with that with the way that we were using the etherpad; add topics

    But-- there is a lot of talking

    "Knowing the curriculum ahead of time would have been good" -- but they *do* get information ahead of time about this stuff

    Maybe there's a better way to present and consolidate information succinctly for busy professionals 

    A: something that came to mind about that piece of feedback - at least half of this group work together, and group dynamics can sometimes get strange; one person has a particular complaint and they get everyone worked up about it

    M: Communication about workshops has changed a little bit -- it might now say that this is an "example" schedule, rather than a solid schedule 

    Two day workshop can be very draining

    One learner asked - have you considered doing a three day workshop? On day 1, rest day, on day 2 

    This seems potentially interesting given that some people might have a M/W/F type schedule instead of a set schedule each day

    Might give them more time to work on their demo and they might feel more prepared

    The 2-day workshop gets more questions about scheduling because it takes so much more of your time 

    Spacing it out more, no matter how much we try to stress that it is low stakes, they would have just had more time to get anxious about it 

    Anxiety about doing demo: focusing on this being an opportunity for getting and giving feedback, NOT an opportunity to show off your skills -- the teaching demo is five minutes of your life, we're building a community here, and it's a community of practice; you want to get better over time. 

    There's a lot of focus on the demo as being a big deal, but it's not even really the important part! 

    Another interesting way to frame around the "three minutes" in the practice training versus the actual demo being "five" minutes 

    There can be a real positive to this anxiety that they experience -- this is building to them teaching -- and they're going to go through that anxiety when they teach, too, but that's going to be even more daunting (esp if they haven't even done the demo and gotten feedback yet!)
